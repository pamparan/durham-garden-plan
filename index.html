Before You Plant

Soil Prep — Daddy Pete’s Products

Your beds are filled with a mix of Daddy Pete’s Raised Bed Mix and Lawn & Garden Soil. Here’s how to amend and finish them for maximum success in Zone 8a.

Step 1 — Loosen & Amend
  1. Loosen the existing soil to a depth of 6-8 inches with a garden fork or hand cultivator.
  2. Spread 2 inches of Daddy Pete’s Mushroom Compost evenly over each bed.
  3. Mix the compost into the top 6 inches — don’t just leave it on the surface.
Why mushroom compost? It loosens the heavier Lawn & Garden Soil, adds potassium and calcium (great for peppers and tomatoes), and improves moisture retention for Zone 8a heat. Keep it under 25-30% of the total mix — it runs slightly alkaline (pH 7-8) and most vegetables prefer 6.0-7.0.
Step 2 — Bed-Specific Prep
  • Bed 1: Dig the tomato hole deep — bury the stem up to the lowest leaves. Mix a handful of compost into the bottom of the hole. Loosen soil along the left edge where squash roots will anchor.
  • Bed 2: Work compost in thoroughly — 6 plants will share nutrients. The lettuce zone (front-left) benefits most from compost’s moisture retention.
  • Bed 3: Peppers love calcium in mushroom compost — prevents blossom end rot. Mix evenly across the whole bed.
  • Bed 4: Cucumbers are heavy feeders. Give this bed a slightly heavier compost ratio, especially on the trellis side (back) where cucumber roots concentrate.
Step 3 — Pine Bark Mulch

After planting, apply 2-3 inches of Daddy Pete’s pine bark mulch on all exposed soil. Non-negotiable in Zone 8a:

  • Keeps soil temperature 10-15° cooler in July/August
  • Reduces watering frequency by 30-50%
  • Suppresses weeds and prevents soil splash (spreads disease)
  • Slightly acidic (pH 5.5-6.5) — helps offset the alkalinity of the mushroom compost, bringing soil closer to ideal 6.0-7.0
  • Keep mulch 2 inches away from plant stems to prevent rot
  • Extend mulch onto ground where vines will trail
  • Use straw (not bark mulch) under developing melons and squash fruit — bark holds too much moisture against fruit skin and causes bottom rot. Cardboard works in a pinch.
Pine bark breaks down slowly and won’t mat down, allowing water and air to penetrate. As Bed 2’s “living mulch” plants fill in, they’ll shade the soil themselves and you’ll need less actual mulch there.
Step 4 — Deep Soak

Once amended, planted, and mulched:

  1. Water each bed until it drains from the bottom
  2. This settles soil around roots and activates the compost
  3. Wait 24 hours, then check moisture — water again if top inch is dry
  4. Shift to regular watering schedule (see Watering Guide)

Between-Bed Strips (6.5" × 32" × 3)

The narrow gaps between beds are planted with companion species that deter pests and attract beneficial insects right where they’re needed most.

Strip 1 — Between Bed 1 & Bed 2
Chives
  • Plant a single row down the 32” strip
  • Allium scent repels aphids from both tomato beds
  • Perennial in 8a — plant once, harvest for years
  • Purple flowers in spring attract pollinators
  • Edible — snip tops for cooking anytime
Strip 2 — Between Bed 2 & Bed 3
Sage (Common)
  • Trim regularly to keep within the 6.5” strip width
  • Strong scent deters flea beetles (protects Bed 2 eggplant) and cabbage moths
  • Perennial in 8a — stays in place year-round, even when beds are cleared
  • Harvest leaves for cooking as you trim — nothing goes to waste
  • Positioned between eggplant (Bed 2) and peppers (Bed 3) for maximum pest defense
Strip 3 — Between Bed 3 & Bed 4
French Marigolds (dwarf)
  • Use compact varieties like ‘Bonanza’ — 8-10” tall, fits the width
  • Repels nematodes, whiteflies, and aphids
  • Placed between peppers and eggplant — maximum pest defense
  • Attracts hoverflies that prey on garden pests
  • Bright orange/yellow blooms all season

Climbing & Structural Support

Three plants need vertical support against the fence. Here’s what each needs.

Bed 1 — Better Boy Tomato
FENCE
Tomato Cage or Stakes
  • Use a sturdy cage (at least 5 ft tall) or stake with ties
  • Place cage at planting time to avoid root damage later
  • Can also tie main stem to fence posts with soft twine
  • Indeterminate — grows 6-8 ft in Zone 8a’s long season
Bed 2 — Yellow Pear Cherry Tomato
FENCE
Tall Cage or Trellis
  • Needs the tallest support — Yellow Pear is extremely vigorous
  • Use a 6 ft cage or trellis panel leaned against the fence
  • Prune aggressively — remove suckers weekly or it will engulf the bed
  • Heavy fruit clusters may need individual support ties
Bed 4 — Cucumber Burpless ×2
FENCE
Fence Trellis or Netting
  • Attach wire mesh, cattle panel, or trellis netting directly to the fence
  • Cucumbers climb with tendrils — they’ll grab the mesh naturally
  • Train vines upward in the first weeks; they’ll self-climb after
  • Vertical growing keeps fruit clean, straight, and easy to spot
  • Space the 2 plants 18-24” apart along the fence
No support needed
Peppers, eggplant, basil, lettuce, and all squash/watermelon are self-supporting or ground-trailing. Pepper stems may benefit from a short stake (12-18”) if heavy with fruit in late season, but it’s optional.
Zone 2

Deck Railing Pots — Kitchen Herb Garden

Three herb railing pots and two strawberry railing pots (~10"×24" each, rounded rectangle) on the deck, separate from the garden beds. Twelve culinary herbs chosen for daily kitchen use and Zone 8a heat tolerance, plus 6 strawberry crowns across 2 pots.

Pot 1 — Everyday Essentials
Layout logic: Thyme and chives anchor the rounded ends (both upright, narrow footprint). Chamomile sits center-back (tallest, airy). Parsley fills center-front (bushy, tolerates the slight shade from chamomile behind it).
Pot 2 — Fresh Flavor & Pickles
Layout logic: Tarragon and oregano anchor the rounded ends (both spread sideways to fill). Dill sits center-back (tallest, feathery — won’t block sun). Cilantro fills center-front (harvest often, resow every 3 weeks since it bolts fast in 8a heat).
Pot 3 — Backups & Bold Flavors
Layout logic: Lemongrass anchors the left end (tallest, doubles as mosquito deterrent). Cilantro #2 staggers with Pot 2’s cilantro so you always have fresh leaves. Curly parsley backs up your flat-leaf. Marjoram fills the right end.

Pot 1 — Everyday Essentials

  • Chamomile — attracts beneficial insects, calming tea. Move to partial shade in peak July heat.
  • Thyme — roasts, soups, mushrooms, pan sauces. Drought-tough, barely needs watering.
  • Parsley (flat-leaf) — the most-used herb in any kitchen. Eggs, pasta, grains, chimichurri, everything.
  • Chives — snip and sprinkle on eggs, potatoes, sour cream, salads. Nearly impossible to kill.

Pot 2 — Fresh Flavor & Pickles

  • Dill — pickle your cucumbers, tzatziki, fish, potato salad. Resow seeds when it bolts.
  • Oregano — pizza, pasta sauce, roasted veggies, marinades. Spreads to fill its space; dries beautifully.
  • Cilantro — tacos, salsa, rice, curries, guac. Bolts fast in heat — resow every 3 weeks.
  • French Tarragon — chicken, eggs, vinaigrettes, béarnaise. Must be bought as a plant (not seeds). Perennial in 8a.

Pot 3 — Backups & Bold Flavors

  • Lemongrass — Thai/Vietnamese curries and soups. Natural mosquito deterrent on the deck. Buy live stalks from H Mart.
  • Cilantro #2 — stagger with Pot 2’s cilantro so one is always ready. Sow 2 weeks offset.
  • Parsley (curly) — different texture from flat-leaf. Great for garnish, tabbouleh, green sauces.
  • Marjoram — sweeter, more delicate than oregano. Pizza, roasted veggies, tomato sauce, soups.

Strawberry Railing Pots

Two railing pots (~10"×24") dedicated to strawberries. The shallow profile works well — strawberries have shallow root systems and runners cascade over the edges.

Strawberry Railing Pot Care

  • Spacing: 3 crowns per pot, ~8" apart. Runners will cascade over the edges naturally.
  • Sun: Full sun (6+ hours). Move to afternoon shade only if leaves scorch in peak July/August.
  • Water: Daily in summer — shallow pots dry fast. Twice daily in extreme heat. Keep soil moist but not waterlogged.
  • Feed: Liquid fertilizer every 2 weeks during fruiting (balanced 10-10-10 or strawberry-specific).
  • First year: Light fruit this season. Pinch early flowers to strengthen roots — heavy production next spring.
  • Winter: Hardy in 8a. Mulch crowns lightly or move pots against the house wall for insulation.

Technique

Trailing Vine Management

Squash and watermelon root at the side edges of the end beds (Bed 1 left, Bed 4 right) and trail outward along the fence line, keeping the front of all beds clear for access.

Prep the Side Zones

Mow the yard areas to the left of Bed 1 and right of Bed 4 short. Lay cardboard or landscape fabric in a 6-8 ft zone extending outward. Cover with straw mulch.

Direct the Vines Weekly

Steer vines away from the beds and outward along the fence line. Keep the front of all beds clear as a walkway for harvesting and watering.

Protect Watermelon Fruit

Once fruit is golf-ball sized, slide cardboard or a small board underneath to prevent bottom rot from wet grass contact.

Mowing Blackout Zones

Two areas become vine territory: the yard to the left of Bed 1 and the right of Bed 4 (6-8 ft out). The front of the beds stays accessible all season.

Care

Watering Guidelines

Zone 8a summers are hot and humid. Consistent watering prevents blossom end rot, bitter cucumbers, and wilting herbs.

PlantsNeedSchedule
Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant Moderate 1-2" per week. Deep water every 2-3 days. Pine bark mulch beds 2-3". Inconsistent watering causes blossom end rot.
Cucumbers High Water daily in peak summer. Pine bark mulch heavily. Never let dry out — drought causes bitter fruit.
Squash, watermelon Moderate Deep soak 2×/week at root crown. Water morning to keep foliage dry. Vines find supplemental moisture.
Lettuce Moderate Daily light watering. Once it bolts, pull it.
Basil Moderate Water when top inch of soil is dry. Don’t let it wilt.
Chamomile & sage (pots) Low Let soil dry slightly between waterings. Sage especially hates wet roots.
Strawberries (pot) High Check daily. May need water 2×/day in 90°F+ heat. Pots dry fast.
Rosemary (front yard) Low Weekly first month, then only during extended drought. Hates wet feet.
Front yard perennials Moderate 2-3×/week first summer to establish. Low-water after. Belarina needs consistent moisture permanently.
