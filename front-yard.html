Fruit Trees

Apple & Pear Trees

Three columnar apple trees and one multi-grafted pear tree. The columnar apples grow only 2 ft wide — perfect for tight spaces or as an edible hedge. Cross-pollinate each other for best yields.

Shade Trees

Red Maple Trees (×2)

Native to North Carolina and a Durham favorite. Fast-growing shade trees with spectacular fall color. Plan their placement carefully — they get large.

Red Maple — Acer rubrum (×2)
DetailInfo

Important: Red maples develop dense mats of shallow surface roots that outcompete anything growing beneath them. It is very difficult to grow other plants under a mature red maple. Plan placement so the eventual canopy doesn't shade your fruit trees or garden beds. Keep at least 20-30 ft from patios, driveways, and sidewalks to prevent root heaving.

Blueberry Row Soil Prep

Durham's native soil is typically pH 5.5-6.5 — too alkaline for blueberries. You must acidify before planting:

  1. Dig each hole 2x the width of the root ball and just as deep
  2. Mix excavated soil 50/50 with sphagnum peat moss or pine bark fines
  3. Add elemental sulfur (1 cup per hole) to lower pH — follow package rates
  4. Plant so the top of the root ball is level with the surrounding soil
  5. Mulch with 2-3 inches of pine bark or pine needles — these maintain acidity as they break down
  6. Water deeply and keep soil consistently moist (like a wrung-out sponge)
Do NOT use mushroom compost for blueberries — it's alkaline (pH 7-8) and will raise soil pH, which is the opposite of what blueberries need. Your Daddy Pete's pine bark mulch is perfect here.

Suggested Row Layout (Side of House)

Pink Lemonade in the center (largest at maturity). Peach Sorbets are compact — space 2-3 ft apart. Leave 4-5 ft between Pink Lemonade and the nearest Peach Sorbet.

A reliable performer in Zone 8a. Needs full sun, heat, and well-drained soil. Could go in either the front or back yard.

First 2 winters: Young pomegranates are less cold-hardy than established ones. Mulch the root zone heavily and wrap the trunk if temps below 15°F are forecast. After year 2, it toughens up significantly. Self-pollinating but bees improve yields — plant near lavender or rosemary.
Placement Guidance

Front Yard (recommended)

  • Pairs perfectly with your lavender and rosemary (same sun/drainage needs)
  • Stunning ornamental — orange-red flowers, glossy leaves, showy fruit
  • Plant near a south-facing wall for extra heat and cold protection

Back Yard

  • Works well near the garden beds if there's a sunny open area
  • Keep 10-15 ft from the raised beds so it doesn't shade them as it matures
  • Avoid planting near the peppers/tomatoes in beds (nightshades can share pests)

Strategy

Companion Planting Guide

Which plants support each other, and which to keep apart. Group plants with similar soil and water needs together.

Mediterranean Group

Plant together — identical needs: full sun, lean well-drained soil, low water.

  • Lavender (Phenomenal + SuperBlue)
  • Rosemary (×2)
  • Pomegranate (if front yard)
Lavender and rosemary attract pollinators that boost pomegranate fruit set. All three are drought-tolerant and hate wet feet. Use gravel mulch, not bark.

Acid-Loving Group

Keep together — all need pH 4.5-5.5 acidic soil. Separate from the Mediterranean group.

  • Peach Sorbet Blueberry (×4)
  • Pink Lemonade Blueberry (×1)
  • Mulch with pine bark or pine needles only
Never use mushroom compost or alkaline amendments near blueberries. The side-yard row isolates them from the front yard's neutral-pH plantings.

Fruit Tree Guild

Companions to plant around the base of your pear and apple trees:

  • Chives — repel aphids and Japanese beetles
  • White clover (ground cover) — fixes nitrogen, living mulch
  • Nasturtiums — trap crop that lures aphids away from fruit
  • Comfrey — deep roots pull up nutrients; chop leaves as mulch
  • Lavender/rosemary — attract pollinators during bloom
Plant companions outside the drip line of young trees to avoid root competition. Ground covers like clover can grow right up to the trunk.

Plants to Keep Apart

Avoid these combinations:

  • Walnut trees near anything — release juglone that inhibits growth
  • Pomegranate near nightshades (tomatoes, peppers) — share pests
  • Lavender near moisture-loving plants — different water needs kill one or the other
  • Blueberries near alkaline-soil plants — conflicting pH requirements
  • Anything under mature red maples — shallow roots outcompete everything
