Squash Vine Borer High Threat
Adult squash vine borer moth — orange body, metallic green wings, wasp-like

How to Identify

Melittia cucurbitae — the #1 killer of squash in Zone 8a

  • Adult moth: Bright orange-red abdomen with black wings, resembles a wasp. Flies during the day (unusual for moths). About 1″ long.
  • Larva: White grub with brown head, 1″ long, found INSIDE the vine stem
  • Eggs: Tiny (1mm), flat, reddish-brown, laid singly at the base of stems
  • Frass: Sawdust-like orange-yellow mush oozing from holes at the stem base — this is the #1 sign you already have them
  • Wilting: Vine suddenly wilts on a hot afternoon despite moist soil — the larva has severed the water flow inside the stem
Jun (1st wave) Aug-Sep (2nd wave) Spaghetti Squash Crookneck Straightneck Watermelon
Plants at Risk
  • High risk: Spaghetti squash, crookneck, straightneck (all Cucurbita)
  • Moderate risk: Watermelon (thinner stems, less preferred)
  • Low risk: Cucumbers (usually too thin-stemmed)
  • Prefers thick, hollow stems — your trailing squash are prime targets
Natural Treatment
  • Surgery: Slit the stem lengthwise with a razor where you see frass. Remove the grub. Bury the wounded stem section in moist soil to encourage re-rooting.
  • Bt injection: Inject Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) into the stem with a syringe at the entry hole
  • Row covers: Cover plants until flowering begins (remove for pollination)
  • Neem oil: Spray stem bases weekly from late May through July
Prevention
  • Wrap stems: Wrap the lower 3-4″ of stems with aluminum foil or nylon stocking to block egg-laying
  • Check stems weekly: Run your fingers along the base feeling for soft spots or frass from early June onward
  • Encourage secondary rooting: Bury vine nodes where they touch soil — if the main stem is severed, the vine survives on secondary roots
  • Succession planting: Plant a second round of squash in early July to replace anything killed by the first wave
  • Fall cleanup: Remove and destroy all spent squash stems — pupae overwinter in the soil beneath them
Tomato Hornworm Medium Threat
Tomato hornworm — 3–4″ green caterpillar with white stripes

How to Identify

Manduca quinquemaculata — impressive but destructive

  • Caterpillar: Massive green caterpillar (3-4″ long) with white V-shaped stripes and a prominent horn on the rear. Masters of camouflage — hard to spot despite their size.
  • Droppings: Large, dark green/black pellets on leaves and ground below — often the first clue. Look UP from the droppings.
  • Damage: Entire branches stripped bare overnight. They eat leaves, stems, and even green fruit.
  • Adult moth: Five-spotted hawk moth, large (4-5″ wingspan), gray-brown, active at dusk. You may see them hovering at flowers like hummingbirds.
  • Parasitized: If you see white cocoons (look like grains of rice) on the caterpillar’s back, LEAVE IT. Those are braconid wasp eggs — the wasp larvae are killing the hornworm and will hatch to protect your garden.
Jun-Aug Better Boy Tomato Yellow Pear Tomato Peppers Eggplant Gretel
Plants at Risk
  • Primary: Both tomatoes (Better Boy, Yellow Pear)
  • Secondary: Eggplant Gretel, all peppers
  • Anything in the nightshade (Solanaceae) family
  • A single hornworm can defoliate a tomato branch in one day
Natural Treatment
  • Hand-pick: The most effective method. Check plants at dusk or dawn when they’re actively feeding. Drop into soapy water.
  • Black light: Hornworms glow under UV/blacklight at night — makes them much easier to find
  • Bt spray: Bacillus thuringiensis var. kurstaki (Btk) — safe, organic caterpillar killer. Spray on foliage, they ingest it and stop feeding within hours.
  • Spinosad: Organic insecticide effective against caterpillars. Apply in evening to protect pollinators.
Prevention
  • Basil companion planting: Your sweet basil in Bed 1 repels the adult hawk moths from laying eggs
  • Attract parasitic wasps: Your chamomile and dill attract braconid wasps that parasitize hornworms
  • Till in fall: Pupae overwinter 3-4″ deep in soil. Turning the soil exposes them to birds and cold.
  • Daily inspection: Look for droppings first, then trace upward. They’re perfectly camouflaged against tomato foliage.
Leave parasitized hornworms alone! White rice-like cocoons on a hornworm’s back are braconid wasp eggs. The wasp larvae are eating the hornworm alive and will hatch as adults that protect your garden for years. Killing a parasitized hornworm kills dozens of your best allies.
Cucumber Beetle Medium Threat
Striped cucumber beetle — yellow with 3 black stripes Spotted cucumber beetle — yellow-green with 12 black spots

How to Identify

Acalymma vittatum (striped) / Diabrotica undecimpunctata (spotted)

  • Striped: 1/4″ long, bright yellow with three black stripes down the back. The more common type in Durham.
  • Spotted: Same size, yellow-green with 12 black spots. Less common but equally destructive.
  • Behavior: Found inside flowers and on young leaves. Jump and fly when disturbed.
  • Damage: Chew holes in leaves, flowers, and fruit rinds. The real danger is they spread bacterial wilt — the plant wilts and dies within days, and there is no cure once infected.
  • Larvae: White, slender grubs in the soil eating roots (rarely noticed).
Jun-Jul Cucumbers All Squash Watermelon
Plants at Risk
  • High risk: Cucumbers (most susceptible to bacterial wilt they carry)
  • Moderate: All squash varieties, watermelon
  • Bacterial wilt transmitted by beetles kills cucumbers within a week — no treatment exists once infected
Natural Treatment
  • Hand-pick: Morning is best, when they’re sluggish. Drop into soapy water.
  • Yellow sticky traps: Place at soil level near cucumbers and squash
  • Neem oil spray: Deters feeding and disrupts reproduction. Apply weekly to foliage.
  • Kaolin clay (Surround): Creates a white film on leaves that confuses and deters beetles
  • Spinosad: Apply in evening only — toxic to bees when wet
Prevention
  • Row covers: Most effective method. Cover seedlings until flowering, then remove for pollination.
  • Trap crops: Plant Blue Hubbard squash nearby — beetles prefer it 10:1 over cucumbers
  • Delay planting: The first beetle wave targets the earliest cucurbits. Later plantings face less pressure.
  • Epazote: Your deck pot of epazote deters cucumber beetles with its strong aromatic oils
Squash Lady Beetle Medium Threat
Squash lady beetle — yellow with 7 black spots per wing, ladybug-shaped

How to Identify

Epilachna borealis — the plant-eating ladybug

  • Size: About 3/8″ (7–10 mm) — larger than most lady beetles. Dome-shaped body.
  • Color: Yellow to orange with seven large black spots on each wing cover (14 total) and four smaller spots on the thorax. Easily mistaken for a beneficial ladybug.
  • Larvae: Yellow, slug-like grubs covered in six rows of dark, branching spines. Found on leaf undersides.
  • Eggs: Bright yellow clusters of 40–75 eggs on leaf undersides — look similar to beneficial ladybug eggs but are on cucurbit leaves.
  • Feeding pattern: Adults and larvae scrape leaf tissue between veins, leaving a distinctive “lacy” or skeletonized look. Before feeding, they trench a semicircle around their target area.
  • Late-season: Adults also chew on squash and pumpkin rind later in the season.
Jun-Sep Crookneck Squash Spaghetti Squash Straightneck Squash Cucumbers Watermelon

Plants at Risk

  • Primary targets: All your squash varieties (crookneck, spaghetti, straightneck) are the main draw — this beetle is named for its squash obsession.
  • Secondary: Cucumbers and watermelon are also cucurbits and will be fed on.
  • Late season: When foliage gets tough, adults move to feeding on fruit rinds, leaving scarred patches on squash.
  • Not at risk: Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, herbs, and fruit trees are not cucurbits and are safe.

Natural Treatment

  • Hand-pick adults: They’re slow-moving and easy to grab — drop into soapy water. Check mornings when they’re sluggish.
  • Crush egg clusters: Check undersides of squash leaves regularly. Yellow egg masses are easy to spot — wipe them off with your thumb.
  • Neem oil spray: Effective against larvae. Spray undersides of leaves where larvae feed. Apply early morning or evening.
  • Spinosad: Organic insecticide that works well on larvae. Apply when you first see the spiny yellow grubs.
  • Row covers: Cover squash plants until flowering begins to block egg-laying adults entirely.

Prevention

  • Don’t confuse with beneficials: Helpful lady beetles are usually red/orange with fewer spots and are found on aphid-infested plants, not squash. The squash beetle is always on cucurbit leaves.
  • Trap crop: Plant a sacrificial Blue Hubbard squash nearby — squash beetles prefer it and will congregate there for easy removal.
  • Clean up debris: Adults overwinter under leaf litter and bark near the garden. Clear old cucurbit vines and mulch edges in fall.
  • Rotate beds: Don’t plant squash in the same bed next year. Even in small gardens, moving cucurbits to a different bed disrupts the beetle’s return to familiar territory.
  • Companion planting: Radishes and nasturtiums near squash can help deter. Your dill (herb pots) attracts parasitic wasps that prey on the larvae.
Flea Beetle Medium Threat
Flea beetle — tiny dark beetle, jumps when disturbed

How to Identify

Epitrix spp. — tiny but persistent

  • Size: Extremely small (1/16″ to 1/8″), dark colored (black, bronze, or metallic blue-green)
  • Behavior: Jump like fleas when disturbed — hence the name. Very fast and hard to catch.
  • Damage: Leaves covered in tiny round “shot holes” that look like someone took a hole punch to them. Heavy feeding makes leaves look lace-like.
  • Worst on seedlings: Established plants tolerate the damage. Young transplants can be killed.
Jun-Sep Eggplant Gretel Peppers (minor)
Plants at Risk
  • Primary: Eggplant Gretel — eggplant is their #1 preferred host
  • Minor: Peppers, tomatoes (usually cosmetic only)
  • Established plants can tolerate significant flea beetle feeding without yield loss
Natural Treatment
  • Neem oil: Spray every 5-7 days, coating both leaf surfaces. Deters feeding and disrupts reproduction.
  • Diatomaceous earth: Dust on dry leaves. The microscopic sharp edges damage beetle exoskeletons. Reapply after rain.
  • Kaolin clay (Surround): White clay film on leaves makes them unpalatable
  • Sticky traps: White or yellow sticky cards near eggplant
Prevention
  • Sage in Strip 2: Your sage between Bed 2 and Bed 3 is positioned exactly right — its strong oils deter flea beetles from the eggplant
  • Row covers: Protect young transplants for the first 2-3 weeks
  • Healthy, fast-growing plants: Well-fertilized eggplant outgrows the damage. Keep feeding.
  • Mulch heavily: Adults overwinter in soil. Thick mulch disrupts their lifecycle.
Aphids Low Threat
Aphids — tiny green clusters on stems and leaf undersides

How to Identify

Aphidoidea — the most common garden pest worldwide

  • Size: Very small (1/16″ to 1/8″), soft-bodied, pear-shaped. Colors vary: green, black, yellow, pink, white.
  • Clusters: Found in dense colonies on new growth, leaf undersides, and flower buds
  • Honeydew: Excrete sticky, shiny residue on leaves. Black sooty mold grows on the honeydew.
  • Curling leaves: Heavy infestations cause new leaves to curl, yellow, and distort
  • Ants: Ants “farm” aphids for honeydew. If you see ant trails on your plants, check for aphid colonies.
All Season Peppers Lettuce Eggplant Apple Trees Pear Tree Red Maples
Plants at Risk
  • Raised beds: Peppers, lettuce, eggplant
  • Front yard: Apple trees, pear tree, red maples
  • Herbs: Rarely bothered — most herbs repel aphids
  • Low threat because natural predators (ladybugs, lacewings) usually keep populations in check
Natural Treatment
  • Strong water blast: Knock them off with a hard spray from the hose. Most effective and simplest method. Repeat every 2-3 days.
  • Insecticidal soap: Spray directly on colonies. Must contact the aphids to work. Reapply after rain.
  • Neem oil: Disrupts feeding and reproduction. Apply weekly as preventive.
  • Release ladybugs: Buy and release at dusk near infested plants. Each eats 50+ aphids per day.
Prevention
  • Your chives (Strip 1): Allium scent repels aphids from adjacent tomato beds
  • Basil in Beds 1 & 2: Aromatic oils confuse aphid navigation
  • Chamomile & dill: Attract hoverflies and lacewings that devour aphids
  • Avoid over-fertilizing: Too much nitrogen produces soft, succulent growth that aphids prefer
  • Don’t kill ladybugs: They’re your best defense. A single ladybug eats thousands of aphids in its lifetime.
Japanese Beetle Medium Threat
Japanese beetle — metallic green head, copper wing covers

How to Identify

Popillia japonica — unmistakable metallic beauty

  • Adult: 1/2″ long, metallic green head and thorax, copper-bronze wing covers. Five tufts of white hair on each side of the abdomen.
  • Damage: “Skeletonize” leaves — eat the tissue between veins, leaving a lace-like skeleton. Feed in groups, starting at the top of the plant.
  • Behavior: Most active on warm, sunny days. Slow-moving in morning — easiest to hand-pick then.
  • Grubs: White C-shaped larvae in lawn soil. Attract moles, skunks, and birds that dig up turf.
Jul-Aug Apple Trees Pear Tree Red Maples Many garden plants
Plants at Risk
  • Front yard: Apple trees, pear tree, red maples, roses
  • Raised beds: Occasional damage to basil, peppers
  • They eat 300+ plant species — almost nothing is safe
  • Damage is mostly cosmetic on established plants but can weaken young trees
Natural Treatment
  • Hand-pick into soapy water: The single most effective method. Do it in the morning when beetles are sluggish. Shake branches over a bucket.
  • Neem oil: Deters feeding and is toxic to beetles when ingested. Apply to foliage.
  • Milky spore: Apply to lawn in fall — kills grubs (the larval stage) in the soil. Takes 2-3 years to establish but lasts 15+ years.
  • Beneficial nematodes: Heterorhabditis bacteriophora — apply to lawn in late summer to kill grubs
Prevention
  • DO NOT use Japanese beetle traps: They attract MORE beetles to your yard than they catch. Your neighbor’s beetles will come to your trap and stay for your garden.
  • Geraniums: Naturally paralyze Japanese beetles (they eat the petals, become disoriented, fall off, and are eaten by predators)
  • Treat the lawn: Milky spore and nematodes in your lawn kill the grub stage before they become adults
  • Early morning patrol: 5 minutes of hand-picking per day during peak season is surprisingly effective
Never use Japanese beetle bag traps. University research consistently shows they attract 3-5x more beetles to your property than they capture. The pheromone lure draws beetles from a wide radius, and many land on your plants instead of entering the bag.
Brown Marmorated Stink Bug Medium Threat
Brown marmorated stink bug — shield-shaped, mottled brown

How to Identify

Halyomorpha halys — invasive, smelly, and destructive

  • Adult: Shield-shaped, mottled brown, about 5/8″ long. Alternating light and dark bands on the antennae and abdomen edges. Releases a strong, unpleasant odor when disturbed or crushed.
  • Nymphs: Smaller, rounder, with red and black coloring when young. Look like tiny ticks on leaves.
  • Eggs: Light green barrel-shaped clusters of 20-30 on leaf undersides
  • Damage: Pierce-and-suck feeding creates dimpled, discolored, corky spots on tomatoes and fruit. Makes fruit unmarketable but still edible — just cut out the damaged spot.
Jul-Oct Tomatoes Peppers Apple Trees Pear Tree
Plants at Risk
  • Raised beds: Tomatoes, peppers (both sweet and hot)
  • Front yard: Apple trees, pear tree
  • Feeds on 100+ host plants
  • Damage is cosmetic — fruit is still safe to eat
Natural Treatment
  • Hand-pick into soapy water: Flick them into a jar of soapy water. Don’t crush them unless you enjoy the smell.
  • Kaolin clay (Surround): White clay barrier on fruit deters feeding
  • Trap crop: Your sunflowers in the front yard are already serving as a trap crop — stink bugs prefer sunflowers over tomatoes
  • Neem oil: Deters feeding but won’t kill adults. More effective on nymphs.
Prevention
  • Sunflower trap crop: Your 4 sunflowers in the front yard draw stink bugs away from the raised beds
  • Eliminate overwintering sites: They hide in houses, sheds, and debris over winter. Seal gaps in your shed.
  • Attract parasitoid wasps: Trissolcus japonicus (samurai wasp) is establishing naturally in NC. Chamomile and dill support beneficial wasps generally.
  • Remove egg masses: Check leaf undersides weekly. Scrape off the barrel-shaped green egg clusters.
Whitefly Low Threat
Whiteflies — tiny white moths on leaf underside, scatter when touched

How to Identify

Trialeurodes vaporariorum / Bemisia tabaci

  • Adults: Tiny (1/16″), white, moth-like insects on leaf undersides. When you brush a plant and a cloud of tiny white specks flies up — those are whiteflies.
  • Nymphs: Flat, translucent, scale-like dots on leaf undersides. Immobile and easy to miss.
  • Honeydew: Like aphids, whiteflies excrete sticky honeydew that leads to black sooty mold.
  • Yellowing: Heavy infestations cause leaf yellowing and premature drop.
Jun-Sep Tomatoes Peppers Eggplant
Plants at Risk
  • Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant — all nightshades
  • Rarely a serious problem outdoors — much worse in greenhouses
  • Natural predators usually keep outdoor populations manageable
Natural Treatment
  • Yellow sticky traps: Highly effective. Whiteflies are attracted to yellow.
  • Insecticidal soap: Must contact the insects directly. Spray undersides of leaves.
  • Neem oil: Disrupts nymph development. Apply weekly.
  • Strong water spray: Same as aphids — blast the undersides of leaves
Prevention
  • Basil and marigolds: Both repel whiteflies. You have basil in the beds and marigolds in Strip 3.
  • Epazote: Your deck pot deters whiteflies with its strong scent
  • Encourage lacewings: Lacewing larvae are voracious whitefly predators. Chamomile and dill attract adult lacewings.
  • Reflective mulch: Aluminum foil strips laid around plants disorient whiteflies
Spider Mites Medium Threat
Spider mites — tiny red/brown specks with fine silk webbing

How to Identify

Tetranychus urticae — thrives in hot, dry conditions

  • Nearly invisible: Barely visible to the naked eye (1/50″). Use a magnifying glass or tap a leaf over white paper — moving specks are mites.
  • Webbing: Fine silk webbing on leaf undersides and between stems. This is the most visible sign of a heavy infestation.
  • Stippling: Leaves develop tiny yellow or white dots (stippling), then turn bronze and dry out.
  • Dusty look: Heavily infested leaves look dusty or dry even when watered.
  • Worst in drought/heat: Populations explode during hot, dry spells in July-August. Durham’s humidity actually slows them somewhat.
Jul-Sep (peak in heat waves) Tomatoes Cucumbers Eggplant
Plants at Risk
  • Tomatoes, cucumbers, eggplant, beans, strawberries
  • Worse on drought-stressed plants
  • Populations can double every 3 days in hot weather — act fast when spotted
Natural Treatment
  • Hard water blast: Spray leaf undersides with a strong hose stream every 2-3 days. This alone can control mild infestations.
  • Insecticidal soap: Must contact the mites. Spray undersides of leaves thoroughly.
  • Neem oil: Suffocates mites on contact and disrupts reproduction. Apply in evening to avoid leaf burn.
  • DO NOT use broad-spectrum insecticides: These kill predatory mites that keep spider mites in check, making the problem much worse.
Prevention
  • Consistent watering: Stressed, dry plants are magnets for spider mites. Your mulched raised beds help.
  • Spray foliage during heat waves: A quick misting of leaves on hot days raises humidity around the plant — mites hate humidity.
  • Don’t kill the predators: Predatory mites, ladybugs, and lacewings eat spider mites. Avoid broad-spectrum sprays.
  • Check weekly in July-August: Tap suspect leaves over a white paper. Any moving dots need immediate attention.
Blossom End Rot Medium Threat
Blossom end rot — dark, sunken patch on fruit bottom

How to Identify

Not a pest or disease — a calcium uptake disorder caused by inconsistent watering

  • Appearance: Dark, sunken, leathery spot on the BOTTOM (blossom end) of the fruit. Starts small, expands to cover half the fruit.
  • Which fruit: Usually the first fruits of the season. Often resolves on its own as the plant’s root system matures.
  • Not a pathogen: You can cut off the bad part and eat the rest. It’s not rotten — just a calcium deficiency in that particular fruit.
  • Cause: The soil almost always has enough calcium. The problem is inconsistent water prevents the roots from absorbing it. Hot spells followed by heavy watering are the classic trigger.
Jul-Aug (worst in heat) Tomatoes Peppers Squash Watermelon
Plants at Risk
  • Most common: Tomatoes (especially large-fruited like Better Boy)
  • Also affects: Peppers, squash, watermelon
  • Worst on the first fruits of the season — later fruit is usually fine
  • Your Daddy Pete’s mushroom compost is a good calcium source
Natural Treatment
  • Fix watering: This is the only real fix. Water deeply and consistently — same amount, same schedule, every time.
  • Remove affected fruit: Pick off BER fruit so the plant redirects energy to healthy fruit
  • Don’t add calcium: Durham soil almost never lacks calcium. Adding eggshells or calcium spray rarely helps because the problem is water, not calcium.
  • Mulch heavily: 2-3″ of pine bark mulch keeps soil moisture consistent — this is the best preventive.
Prevention
  • Consistent watering: Same amount every 2-3 days. Never let soil dry out then flood it.
  • Mulch: Your pine bark mulch evens out soil moisture between waterings
  • Don’t over-prune roots: Aggressive cultivation near tomato roots damages them and causes BER
  • Avoid excess nitrogen: Too much nitrogen pushes leaf growth over fruit development, worsening calcium competition
The eggshell myth: Crushed eggshells take years to break down in soil. They will not prevent blossom end rot this season. Consistent watering and mulching will. Save the eggshells for your compost pile.
Gloomy Scale High Threat
Gloomy scale on red maple — gray-brown disc-shaped crusts on bark

How to Identify

Melanaspis tenebricosa — #1 pest of red maples in Durham/Raleigh

  • Scale covers: Tiny (~2mm) gray-brown disc-shaped bumps encrusting bark. Scrape with your thumbnail — a pink insect underneath confirms gloomy scale.
  • Bark texture: Heavily infested bark looks rough, bumpy, like a rash. Branches feel gritty.
  • Canopy decline: Thinning canopy, smaller leaves, branch dieback — especially in the upper crown
  • Sooty mold: Black coating on bark and leaves from honeydew excretion
  • Heat connection: NC State research proves trees near pavement and roads (like yours near S Roxboro) have dramatically higher scale populations due to radiated heat stress
Year-round (crawlers Jun-Jul) Red Maples ×2
Plants at Risk
  • Primary: Your two established red maples
  • Red maple (Acer rubrum) is the most susceptible host
  • Does not spread to fruit trees, blueberries, or garden plants
  • Worse on heat-stressed trees near impervious surfaces like roads
Natural Treatment
  • Deep watering 2x/week: NC State research — single most effective long-term treatment. Reduces heat stress that drives scale populations.
  • Dormant oil (Feb): Horticultural oil at dormant rate. Coat trunk and branches to smother overwintering scale.
  • Power wash (Feb): Low-pressure wash on dormant trunk physically dislodges scale covers.
  • Crawler sprays (Jun-Jul): Horticultural oil or insecticidal soap every 7-10 days during crawler emergence (mid-June peak in Durham).
Prevention
  • Mulch the root zone: 2-3″ donut-shaped mulch ring. Reduces heat and retains moisture.
  • Avoid high-nitrogen fertilizer: Promotes soft growth that scale prefer
  • Plant buffer between trees and road: Groundcovers or mulch beds reduce radiated heat from S Roxboro pavement
  • Takes 2-3 years: Consistent multi-season treatment is required. Goal is suppression, not elimination.

Fire Blight High Threat
Fire blight — blackened shoot tips curled into shepherd’s crook

How to Identify

Erwinia amylovora — bacterial disease, biggest threat to pear and apple trees

  • Shepherd’s crook: Shoot tips blacken, wilt, and curl into a distinctive hooked shape. This is the hallmark sign.
  • Bacterial ooze: Amber-colored sticky droplets on infected branches — highly contagious. Rain and insects spread it to other branches.
  • Blackening: Leaves and blossoms turn black and stay attached (don’t fall off). Looks like the branch was scorched by fire.
  • Cankers: Sunken, dark, wet-looking areas on branches and trunk. Bark may crack around the canker edges.
  • Trigger: Warm (65-85°F), wet spring weather — exactly what Durham gets in April-May.
Spring (warm + wet) 5-in-1 Pear Tree Columnar Apples ×3
Plants at Risk
  • High risk: 5-in-1 European pear tree (pears are more susceptible than apples)
  • Moderate risk: All three columnar apples
  • Multi-grafted trees are especially vulnerable — infection can spread from one graft to another
Natural Treatment
  • Prune immediately: Cut 12″ below visible infection into healthy wood. Sterilize pruners between EVERY cut with 10% bleach or 70% rubbing alcohol.
  • Bag and trash cuttings: Never compost fire blight material
  • Copper spray: Apply at 10% bloom and again at 50% bloom as preventive
  • Trunk cankers: If infection reaches the main trunk, consult a certified arborist. Do not attempt trunk surgery yourself.
Prevention
  • Avoid overhead watering: Water at the base only. Splashing water spreads bacteria.
  • Low nitrogen: High-nitrogen fertilizer promotes the succulent growth that blight prefers
  • Don’t prune when wet: Never prune during or right after rain
  • Air circulation: Keep guild plants clear of the trunk base
  • Remove mummified fruit: Bacteria overwinter in dried fruit left on the tree or ground
The pruner sterilization rule is absolute. Fire blight bacteria on your pruners will infect every subsequent cut you make. Dip or spray with 10% bleach or 70% alcohol between EVERY SINGLE CUT — not just between trees, between cuts on the same tree.
